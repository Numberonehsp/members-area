### SLIDE 2 — Setting up the app — calorie goal first, ignore macros for now

When you first open MyFitnessPal it's going to ask you a lot of questions — your goal, your activity level, how much you want to lose per week. Fill these in honestly and it'll generate a calorie target for you.

Here's the key thing for now: focus only on the calorie number. Ignore the macro percentages — the protein, carbs, and fat split — for the moment. We cover macros properly in Module 3. If you try to hit both a calorie target and specific macros at the same time when you're just starting out, it gets overwhelming fast and most people quit.

One number. Calories. That's your only job for now.

If you did the estimate from Module 1 and you want to use that number instead of the app's suggestion, you can override it. Go to Goals in the app settings and enter your calorie target manually. Your own calculation is often more accurate than the app's default, which tends to be on the low side.

### SLIDE 3 — How to log a meal — barcode scanner, search, custom entries

There are three main ways to log food in MyFitnessPal and once you know all three, logging becomes genuinely quick.

The fastest is the barcode scanner. For any packaged food — cereal, yoghurt, protein bars, anything with a label — just tap the camera icon, scan the barcode, enter the amount you're having, and it's logged. Most products in the database are already there. Takes about ten seconds.

For whole foods, restaurant meals, or anything without a barcode, use the search function. Type "chicken breast" or "porridge" and you'll get a list of options. Look for entries marked as Verified — those have been checked for accuracy. Enter your weight in grams if you can, otherwise use the closest size option.

For meals you make regularly at home, it's worth building a custom recipe. Add all the ingredients once, name it, and you can log it in one tap every time after that. Takes five minutes to set up and saves you that time every single week.

Weigh your food where you can, especially for calorie-dense items like oils, nuts, cheese, and peanut butter. A portion that looks like 30 grams is often 50 or 60. We'll come back to this.

### SLIDE 4 — The three things people forget to log

Here's where most people's calorie counts fall apart, without them realising it.

Number one: drinks. Milk in your coffee, fruit juice, a glass of wine, a smoothie — these all have calories, sometimes a lot of them. A latte can be 200 calories. A large glass of orange juice is around 150. If you're drinking anything other than water, black coffee, or plain tea, log it.

Number two: cooking oil. A tablespoon of olive oil is around 120 calories. If you're cooking with oil every day and not logging it, that's potentially 500 to 700 calories a week you're not accounting for. If you don't want to measure oil precisely, at least log a rough estimate — even "1 tsp olive oil" gets you close enough.

Number three: sauces and condiments. Ketchup, mayo, salad dressing, sriracha, pesto. These seem small but they add up quickly, especially if you're generous. A tablespoon of mayonnaise is about 100 calories. Two tablespoons of salad dressing can easily be 150. Log them.

These three categories alone — drinks, oil, condiments — account for the difference between someone who thinks they're eating 1,800 calories and is actually eating 2,200.

### SLIDE 5 — How accurate is it? Managing expectations honestly

MyFitnessPal is not perfectly accurate, and it's worth being honest about that.

The database is user-submitted, which means some entries are wrong. Portion sizes in restaurants are guesses. Home cooking is an estimate. Your body's actual calorie absorption can vary depending on the food, how it's cooked, and your own gut bacteria.

So you might think you're eating 1,900 calories and you're actually eating 2,100. Or 1,750. That variation is normal.

Does that mean tracking is pointless? No. Because even an imperfect picture is vastly better than no picture at all. The goal isn't to know your calories to the nearest digit — it's to get into the right ballpark consistently. And even imprecise tracking builds an awareness of food quantities that most people simply don't have until they start.

The way to work around inaccuracy is simple: watch the results on the scale over two to three weeks, not just the numbers in the app. If you're hitting your calorie target but your weight isn't moving, either your TDEE estimate is off or there are hidden calories somewhere. Adjust the target, not your trust in the process.

### SLIDE 6 — Building the logging habit — the 80% rule

The most common question I hear about tracking is: "Do I have to do this forever?"

The honest answer is: no. Most people track actively for a few months, build a really solid understanding of what they're eating, and then naturally need to check the app less often because they've internalised the rough calorie values of the foods they eat regularly.

But to get to that point, you need to build the habit first. And the best way to do that is with an 80% rule: aim to log 80% of what you eat, not 100%.

Why? Because if you miss a meal or forget to log something, with a 100% mindset you feel like you've failed and sometimes give up entirely. With an 80% mindset, a missed entry is just a missed entry. You carry on. Perfect is the enemy of consistent.

The best time to log is immediately after eating, or even before — logging before a meal actually makes you more thoughtful about what you're having. Some people log the whole day in the morning as a plan, then adjust as the day changes. Find the rhythm that works for you.

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 7 — Key takeaway

The app is a tool to build awareness — not a life sentence.

You don't have to use it forever. You don't have to be perfect with it. You just have to use it consistently enough to learn what you're actually eating.

Most people who track even imperfectly for eight to twelve weeks come out the other side with an intuitive understanding of calories that they never had before. That understanding stays with you even when you stop tracking.
