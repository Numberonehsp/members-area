### SLIDE 2 — What is a calorie?

A calorie is simply a unit of energy. That's it.

It's not good, it's not bad, it's not something to be afraid of. It's just a way of measuring how much energy is in the food and drink you consume — the same way kilometres measure distance or kilograms measure weight.

Your body runs on energy, constantly. Even right now, sitting still, your heart is beating, your lungs are breathing, your brain is thinking — all of that costs energy. That energy comes from the food you eat, measured in calories.

When people say "calories" they almost always mean kilocalories — or kcal — which is what you'll see on food labels. For our purposes, the terms are interchangeable. Calories and kcal mean the same thing.

So: calories in, energy available. That's the foundation of everything else we're going to cover.

---

### SLIDE 3 — What is TDEE?

TDEE stands for Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It's a term that sounds complicated but the concept is simple — it's just the total number of calories your body burns in a day.

That number is made up of four things:

First, your Basal Metabolic Rate — the energy your body burns just to stay alive. Breathing, circulation, keeping your organs running. This accounts for roughly 60 to 70 percent of everything you burn in a day, even if you never move.

Second, the energy you burn through physical activity — everything from a gym session to walking to the shops.

Third, the thermic effect of food — yes, digesting food actually burns calories. Protein especially requires quite a bit of energy to process.

And fourth, general movement that isn't exercise — fidgeting, standing, taking the stairs. This is called NEAT, non-exercise activity thermogenesis, and it adds up more than most people realise.

Add all four together and you get your TDEE — the number of calories your body uses in a day. This is the number that matters.

---

### SLIDE 4 — What a calorie deficit means and why it drives fat loss

Now that you know your TDEE, understanding a calorie deficit is straightforward.

If you eat fewer calories than your TDEE, your body needs to find energy from somewhere else. That somewhere else is stored body fat. Your body breaks down fat and uses it as fuel. Over time, you lose fat. That's fat loss, in its simplest form.

If you eat more than your TDEE, your body has more energy than it needs. The surplus gets stored — primarily as body fat. That's weight gain.

If you eat roughly the same as your TDEE, your weight stays roughly the same. That's maintenance.

This is the energy balance equation. It's not the only factor in body composition — things like protein intake, sleep, and training all matter — but it is the foundational one. You cannot consistently lose fat without being in a calorie deficit over time. Everything else builds on top of this.

A typical fat loss deficit is somewhere between 300 and 500 calories below your TDEE per day. That's enough to drive steady fat loss without leaving you exhausted, starving, or unable to train properly.

### SLIDE 5 — Why precision isn't the goal — consistency is

Here's where a lot of people go wrong. They find out what their calorie target is, and then they try to hit it exactly. Every day. No exceptions.

That's not necessary, and trying to do it often backfires. Calorie tracking apps are estimates. Food labels are estimates. The calories your body actually absorbs can vary. None of this is a precise science, and treating it like one tends to create a lot of stress for very little extra benefit.

What actually works is consistency over time. Being roughly in the right range, most of the time.

Think of it this way: if your target is 1,800 calories, hitting 1,750 or 1,850 makes no meaningful difference. Hitting 1,800 six days out of seven is vastly better than being perfect for three days and abandoning it entirely on day four because you ate a biscuit.

Eighty percent adherence over months produces real results. A hundred percent adherence for two weeks and then giving up produces nothing.

Your goal with calorie tracking isn't perfection. It's awareness, and then consistent, imperfect action.

### SLIDE 6 — Quick-start: how to estimate your rough daily target

Right, let's make this practical. Here's a simple way to get a rough calorie target to start with.

Take your body weight in kilograms and multiply it by a number based on your activity level.

If you're mostly sedentary — desk job, not much exercise — multiply by 28 to 30.
If you're lightly active — some exercise two or three times a week — multiply by 31 to 33.
If you're moderately active — training four or five times a week — multiply by 34 to 36.

So for example, if you weigh 80 kilograms and you're lightly active, your rough TDEE might be around 80 multiplied by 32, which gives you 2,560 calories. A modest fat loss deficit from there would put you at around 2,100 to 2,200 calories per day.

This is not a perfect number. It's a starting point. You'll track, observe what happens to your weight over two to three weeks, and adjust from there. If you're losing weight too fast, eat a bit more. If nothing's moving, bring it down a little. Your body will tell you.

The number isn't the destination — it's the starting point for an honest conversation between you and your body.

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 7 — Key takeaway

Consistency beats precision.

You don't need to count every calorie perfectly. You need to know roughly what you're eating, roughly what your body needs, and then stay roughly in that range more often than not.

That awareness alone — just knowing the numbers — changes everything. Most people are surprised by what they find when they start tracking. Not because they're eating badly, but because they're eating more than they realise.

The goal for this week is simple: find out what you're actually eating. That's it.
