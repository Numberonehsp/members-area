# LESSON 1 — What are macros and why do they matter?
**Quiz:** No

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Every food you eat fits into one of three categories. Just three.

Once you know those three categories — and why each one matters — you'll never look at a food label, a meal plan, or a nutrition article the same way again. Everything that used to sound complicated starts to make sense.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson we're going to define all three macronutrients, explain what each one does in your body, and show you why the ratio matters — not just the total calories.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Protein, carbs, fat defined; calories per gram

The three macronutrients are protein, carbohydrates, and fat. Every food that contains calories contains at least one of these. Most foods contain a mix.

Here's the calorie value of each:

Protein contains 4 calories per gram.
Carbohydrates contain 4 calories per gram.
Fat contains 9 calories per gram — more than double.

This is why fat-dense foods like nuts, cheese, and oils are so calorie-dense. A small handful of almonds can be 200 calories. That's not a bad thing — it just means your portions of fat-heavy foods have a bigger calorie impact than you might expect.

Each macronutrient serves a different purpose in the body. Protein builds and repairs tissue. Carbohydrates are your primary fuel source. Fat supports hormone production and allows your body to absorb certain vitamins. You need all three. We'll go deeper on each one in the lessons ahead.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Why ratios matter, not just total calories

You already know that total calories determine whether you lose, gain, or maintain weight. So why do macros matter?

Because the same number of calories can produce very different results depending on where those calories come from.

Here's a simple example. Imagine two people both eating 1,800 calories a day. Person A gets most of their calories from protein and vegetables with some carbohydrates. Person B gets most of their calories from bread, pasta, and snack foods with very little protein.

Both are in a calorie deficit. But Person A is going to retain more muscle, feel more full, and lose more fat relative to muscle. Person B is likely to lose a mix of fat and muscle, feel hungrier, and have less energy for training.

Same calories. Very different outcomes.

The distribution of your calories across protein, carbs, and fat — your macro ratio — affects your body composition, your hunger levels, your energy, and your training performance. Getting calories right is the foundation. Getting macros right is the next layer.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 4 — Key takeaway

Every food you eat is made of protein, carbohydrates, fat, or a combination of all three. Total calories determine your weight. Macro ratios determine your body composition.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

# LESSON 2 — Protein — the most important macro
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

If you had to optimise one thing in your diet — just one — it would be protein.

More protein protects your muscle while you're in a calorie deficit. It keeps you fuller for longer than carbs or fat. It has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient, meaning your body actually burns more calories digesting it. And it's the one macro most people are consistently under-eating.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you're going to learn what protein actually does in the body, how much you need based on your weight, and the best food sources to hit that target without overthinking it.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Muscle repair and why protein is the priority

Every time you train, you create small tears in your muscle fibres. Your body repairs those tears using amino acids — the building blocks of protein. When the repair process completes, the muscle comes back slightly stronger and slightly larger. That's muscle growth.

Without enough protein, that repair process is compromised. You train, you create damage, and your body doesn't have the raw materials to rebuild properly. You recover more slowly, make less progress, and in a calorie deficit you're at risk of losing muscle mass rather than just fat.

Protein is also the most satiating macronutrient. High-protein meals keep you full longer than equivalent-calorie meals of carbs or fat. This isn't just anecdotal — it's well documented in the research. For anyone trying to eat in a calorie deficit, satiety is everything. The more full you feel on fewer calories, the easier the whole process becomes.

---

### SLIDE 3 — The 1.6 to 2.2g/kg target

The research on protein intake for body composition converges on a fairly clear recommendation: somewhere between 1.6 and 2.2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day.

The lower end of that range, around 1.6g/kg, is sufficient for muscle retention during a calorie deficit and general muscle building. The higher end, around 2g/kg or above, tends to be more beneficial if you're in a significant calorie deficit, you're training hard, or you're getting older.

A simple starting point for most people: aim for around 1.8 to 2 grams per kilogram. So if you weigh 75 kilograms, that's 135 to 150 grams of protein per day.

If that number sounds high compared to what you're currently eating, you're in good company. Most people who start tracking are eating 60 to 80 grams of protein a day when they need double that. The good news is that increasing protein is one of the most effective nutritional changes you can make, and most people find it relatively easy once they know what to eat.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Best sources of protein

The best protein sources are those that provide a lot of protein relative to their total calories. These are sometimes called lean protein sources.

At the top of the list: chicken breast, turkey, white fish (cod, haddock, tilapia), eggs and egg whites, fat-free Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, and tuna.

Red meat like beef and lamb is also high in protein but higher in fat, so the calorie cost per gram of protein is higher. Still a great option — just be mindful of portions.

For plant-based protein: tofu, tempeh, edamame, legumes (lentils, chickpeas, black beans), and high-protein dairy alternatives. Plant proteins tend to be less bioavailable than animal proteins, meaning your body absorbs slightly less of what's listed on the label. If you're plant-based, aim for the higher end of the range — around 2 to 2.2g/kg — and consider combining different protein sources to ensure you're getting all essential amino acids.

Protein powder — whey, casein, or plant-based — is a convenient supplement when whole food protein sources aren't practical. We cover this properly in Module 7.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Aim for 1.6 to 2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day. This single habit — hitting your protein target — will do more for your body composition than almost any other nutritional change you can make.

---

Your action step: calculate your protein target using your current body weight and the 1.8g/kg guideline. Then look at your last three days of food logs and compare what you're actually eating against that target. Most people find a significant gap. That gap is the opportunity.

# LESSON 3 — Carbohydrates — fuel, not fear
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Carbohydrates have had terrible PR for the last thirty years.

Low-carb. No-carb. Keto. Paleo. Every few years there's a new version of the same message: carbs are the enemy. And for most people, that message has created a lot of unnecessary guilt, restriction, and confusion around perfectly healthy foods.

Here's the truth: carbohydrates are your body's preferred fuel source. Especially for exercise. The question isn't whether to eat them — it's which ones, how much, and when.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you're going to understand what carbohydrates actually do, why your body needs them for training and recovery, and why low-carb approaches aren't the right choice for everyone.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Carbohydrates as the primary energy source

When you eat carbohydrates, your body breaks them down into glucose — a simple sugar — and releases it into the bloodstream. Glucose is used immediately for energy, or stored in your muscles and liver as glycogen — a ready reserve of fuel for later.

Glycogen is particularly important for training. When you do any moderate to high intensity exercise — lifting weights, running, cycling, most team sports — your body relies heavily on glycogen for fuel. Low glycogen means reduced performance: heavier sets feel impossible, you fatigue earlier, and recovery is slower.

This is why athletes don't go low-carb before competition. And it's why people who dramatically cut carbs often feel terrible in the gym for weeks until their body adapts.

Your brain also runs almost exclusively on glucose. Which is why very low-carb diets often come with a period of brain fog, difficulty concentrating, and mood changes — particularly in the first two to three weeks. The brain is essentially starving for its preferred fuel.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Glycogen, timing, and carb quality

Not all carbohydrates behave the same way in the body.

Refined carbohydrates — white bread, sugary cereals, sweets, pastries, most processed snack foods — are digested quickly. They cause a sharp rise in blood sugar followed by a faster crash. They're also typically low in fibre and micronutrients, which means they don't keep you full for long.

Complex carbohydrates — oats, sweet potato, brown rice, whole grain bread, legumes, most vegetables — are digested more slowly. They provide a more sustained release of energy, contain significantly more fibre, and tend to be more filling per calorie.

From a calorie perspective, 100 grams of carbohydrate is 100 grams of carbohydrate regardless of the source. But from a satiety, performance, and nutrient perspective, complex carbohydrates are far superior.

Practical guidance: build most of your carbohydrate intake around whole food sources. Oats at breakfast. Rice, potatoes, or pasta with meals. Fruit throughout the day. Save refined carbs for occasional enjoyment, not daily habits.

On timing: having a carbohydrate-containing meal one to two hours before training helps maintain performance. Having carbohydrates within a few hours of training helps replenish glycogen and supports recovery. You don't need to be obsessive about this, but it's useful context if you're training hard.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Why low-carb isn't for everyone

Low-carb diets can work very well for some people. They reduce appetite in many individuals, they can be simpler to follow — fewer decisions around food — and some people genuinely feel better on them. This is real and valid.

But low-carb is not necessary for fat loss. Fat loss requires a calorie deficit. You can achieve a calorie deficit eating plenty of carbohydrates. The research is clear on this: when calories and protein are matched, low-carb and higher-carb diets produce similar fat loss outcomes.

Where low-carb tends to underperform is in people doing resistance training, high-intensity exercise, or team sports. Glycogen is the primary fuel for those activities, and chronically low glycogen means chronically reduced performance, which means less muscle stimulus, which means slower body composition changes over time.

The bottom line: if you enjoy carbohydrates, you don't have to cut them to lose fat. Eat them from quality sources, keep your total calories in check, hit your protein target, and you will make progress.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Carbohydrates are fuel, not the enemy. You don't need to cut them to lose fat — you need to eat them from whole food sources, in the right amount for your total calorie target.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

Your action step: identify the three main sources of carbohydrates in your current diet. Are they mostly refined or mostly whole food sources? One small change — swapping white bread for whole grain, or cereal for porridge — is a meaningful upgrade without any calorie counting required.

# LESSON 4 — Fats — essential, not the enemy
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

If carbohydrates had bad PR, fat had a worse decade. The low-fat movement of the 1980s and 90s convinced an entire generation that fat made you fat. Low-fat yoghurt, low-fat biscuits, low-fat everything — except most of those products replaced fat with sugar and people got less healthy, not more.

The science has caught up. Fat is essential. Your body cannot function properly without it. The question is the same as with carbs: which types, and how much.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you'll learn what dietary fat actually does in your body, why you can't cut it too low, and how to build a diet that includes the right kinds of fat without going over your calorie target.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Fat and hormones

Dietary fat plays a critical role in hormone production.

Testosterone, oestrogen, cortisol, and other steroid hormones are synthesised from cholesterol — which comes from fat in the diet. If you cut dietary fat too low — roughly below 20 percent of your total calories — hormone production can decline. In men this can manifest as reduced testosterone, lower energy, reduced libido, and impaired recovery. In women it can disrupt the menstrual cycle and affect mood and bone health.

This is one of the most common but least discussed problems with very aggressive calorie restriction. When people try to eat as little as possible to lose fat as fast as possible, they often cut fat too low, which suppresses hormones, which undermines training adaptation, recovery, and wellbeing.

The minimum healthy fat intake for most people is around 0.5 to 1 gram per kilogram of body weight per day. So for an 80kg person, that's a minimum of 40 to 80 grams of fat daily. Most people should be in this range or above.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Fat-soluble vitamins and satiety

Beyond hormones, fat serves two other important functions.

First, fat-soluble vitamins. Vitamins A, D, E, and K are absorbed only in the presence of dietary fat. If you eat a salad with no dressing and no fat source, you absorb very little of the vitamins in that salad. This is another reason very low-fat diets can lead to micronutrient deficiencies over time, even when the rest of the diet is healthy.

Second, satiety. Fat slows gastric emptying — meaning food moves more slowly from your stomach to your small intestine. This makes you feel fuller for longer after a meal. Protein has a similar effect. Meals that combine protein and fat tend to be significantly more satiating than meals built primarily around carbohydrates.

This doesn't mean loading your meals with fat — remember, fat is 9 calories per gram, so it adds up quickly. It means including fat-containing whole foods as part of balanced meals rather than avoiding fat out of fear.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Types of fat and good sources

Not all fats are equal. There are three main categories.

Monounsaturated fats — found in olive oil, avocado, almonds, cashews, and most nuts. These are associated with heart health and anti-inflammatory effects. Make these your primary fat source.

Polyunsaturated fats — including omega-3 and omega-6 fatty acids. Omega-3s in particular — found in oily fish like salmon, mackerel, and sardines, and in walnuts, chia seeds, and flaxseed — are strongly linked to reduced inflammation, improved recovery, and cardiovascular health. Most people are under-eating omega-3s.

Saturated fats — found in red meat, butter, cheese, coconut oil, and most animal products. These are fine in moderation. The older advice to eliminate them entirely has been revised; the current evidence suggests moderate saturated fat intake, as part of an otherwise healthy diet, is not harmful.

Trans fats — found in some processed and fried foods. These are the only fats you genuinely want to limit as much as possible. Most food manufacturers have removed them but they still appear in some ultra-processed products.

Practical approach: cook with olive oil, eat oily fish two to three times per week, include nuts or avocado as snacks or in meals, and don't stress too much about occasional saturated fat in whole foods.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Fat is essential — for hormones, vitamin absorption, and satiety. Don't cut it too low. Focus on unsaturated fats from whole food sources, include omega-3s regularly, and keep saturated fat moderate.

---

# LESSON 5 — Setting your macro targets
**Quiz:** No

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

You now understand protein, carbohydrates, and fat. In this final lesson, we're making it practical.

We're going to set actual numbers, put them into MyFitnessPal, and give you a clear priority order so you know exactly what to focus on first.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

By the end of this lesson you'll have a complete macro target set up in the app, and you'll understand why we set them in a specific order rather than all at once.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — The priority order: protein first

Here's the order to set your macros, and more importantly, the order to focus on hitting them.

Step one: protein. Set this first and treat it as the non-negotiable target. Everything else builds around it.

Use 1.8 to 2 grams per kilogram of body weight as your target. So for a 75kg person, that's 135 to 150 grams of protein per day. Enter this number in MyFitnessPal under Goals → Nutrient Goals → Protein.

Why protein first? Because protein has the most evidence behind it for body composition. Getting protein right while being roughly on target with total calories will produce the majority of your results. You can worry about optimising carbs and fats later.

Step two: fat. Set a minimum of 0.6 to 0.8 grams per kilogram of body weight. For a 75kg person, that's around 45 to 60 grams of fat per day. Think of this as your floor, not a target to maximise.

Step three: carbohydrates fill the rest. Once you've allocated calories to protein and fat, whatever is left in your calorie budget goes to carbohydrates. This is your flexible macro — the one that adjusts up or down depending on your total calories and food choices on any given day.

---

### SLIDE 3 — How to set targets in MyFitnessPal

In MyFitnessPal, go to the More tab and select Goals. Then tap Nutrient Goals.

You'll see sliders or input fields for protein, carbs, and fat as percentages. The issue with percentages is that they move relative to total calories, which makes it harder to hit a consistent gram target. If your app allows it, switch to grams — it's much easier to track.

Enter your protein target in grams. Enter your fat minimum in grams. The app will calculate the remaining calories and assign them to carbohydrates.

Once these are set, your daily diary will show you how close you are to each target alongside your calorie total. In the beginning, just focus on the calorie total and protein. When both of those feel comfortable as habits — usually after four to six weeks — you can start paying attention to fat and carbs as well.

Don't try to optimise everything at once. Build habits in layers.

---

### SLIDE 4 — What to do when you can't hit all three

You will have days where hitting every macro target alongside your calorie target feels impossible. That's normal and it's fine.

On those days, the priority order is: calories first, protein second, fat minimum third, carbs last.

If you can only focus on one thing, make it protein. High-protein days are forgiving — they tend to keep you fuller, support your training, and make it easier to stay in a calorie deficit even when the day goes sideways.

Over time, as you become more familiar with the calorie and protein content of the foods you eat regularly, hitting your targets will require much less effort. In the early weeks it feels like a lot. By month two it becomes fairly automatic.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Set protein first. Fill fat to your minimum. Let carbs take the rest. Then focus on hitting calories and protein every day — and let the rest follow.

---
