### SLIDE 2 — What IF actually is (eating windows, not starvation)

Intermittent fasting is not a diet. It's a timing strategy.

It doesn't tell you what to eat — it tells you when to eat. You restrict your eating to a defined window of time each day and fast for the rest. During the fasting window you can drink water, black coffee, and plain tea. Nothing that contains significant calories.

That's it. No special foods, no supplements required, no complex rules about what you can combine. Just a defined eating window.

Why does this work for fat loss? Primarily because it makes it easier for many people to eat fewer calories overall. If you compress your eating into an eight-hour window instead of a sixteen-hour window, you tend to eat fewer meals and consume fewer total calories — not because of any metabolic magic, but because there's simply less opportunity to eat.

Some research suggests fasting periods may have additional benefits around insulin sensitivity and cellular repair processes. The evidence on these mechanisms is interesting but not yet conclusive enough to be the primary reason to choose IF. The main benefit for most people is calorie management through structured eating windows.

---

### SLIDE 3 — The three main protocols: 16:8, 14:10, 5:2

There are three protocols you'll hear about most often.

**16:8** is the most common. You fast for 16 hours and eat within an 8-hour window. The simplest way to do this is to skip breakfast and start eating at noon. You eat lunch, an afternoon snack if needed, and dinner by 8pm. Then you fast from 8pm until noon the next day. Most of that fast happens while you're sleeping.

**14:10** is the gentler version. 14-hour fast, 10-hour eating window. You might eat from 9am to 7pm, for example. This is a good entry point if 16:8 feels too restrictive initially, or if you work physical jobs where you need more fuel in the morning.

**5:2** is structured differently. You eat normally five days a week and restrict calories severely — typically to 500 to 600 calories — on two non-consecutive days. The fasting days are not zero calories; they're very low calorie days. Some people find this easier than daily restrictions. Others find the very-low-calorie days miserable and unsustainable.

All three approaches have solid research supporting their effectiveness for fat loss when adherence is maintained. The best protocol is the one you can actually stick to.

### SLIDE 4 — Who benefits most — and who should be careful

Intermittent fasting tends to work particularly well for a specific type of person.

It suits people who aren't hungry in the morning and find breakfast optional rather than essential. Skipping breakfast feels natural to them and compressing their eating window doesn't increase hunger much. For these people, IF can significantly simplify calorie management — fewer decisions, less time thinking about food.

It also works well for people who struggle with constant grazing throughout the day. Having a defined eating window creates structure that removes the "should I eat this?" decision dozens of times a day. The window is closed, so the answer is always no.

However, there are groups who should approach IF with caution or skip it entirely.

Anyone with a history of disordered eating should consult a healthcare professional before restricting eating windows — IF can reinforce harmful patterns for some people.

People who train early in the morning, particularly at high intensity, often find their performance suffers significantly when fasted. If your best training window is 6am, eating your first meal at noon means training in a severely depleted state. The training quality cost may outweigh the calorie management benefit.

Women should also be aware that some research suggests fasted states can affect hormonal balance more significantly than in men, particularly when combined with heavy training and aggressive calorie restriction. This doesn't mean women shouldn't do IF — many do it successfully — but it's worth monitoring how you feel over several weeks and adjusting if energy, mood, or menstrual cycle changes arise.

People with diabetes or who take medication that requires food should consult their doctor before starting any fasting protocol.

### SLIDE 5 — The first two weeks: side effects and why they pass

If you decide to try intermittent fasting, the first two weeks are often the hardest.

You'll likely experience hunger during your fasting window — particularly in the mornings initially. This is mostly habitual. Your body is used to receiving food at certain times. When it doesn't, it signals hunger. Over time, as your body adapts to the new pattern, those hunger signals reduce significantly. Most people report that after ten to fourteen days, they genuinely aren't hungry in the mornings anymore.

You may also experience headaches, irritability, or difficulty concentrating in the first week, particularly if you're used to eating regularly throughout the day. Staying well hydrated — and making sure you're not also dropping calories too aggressively at the same time — helps considerably.

A practical tip: if you're starting 16:8, push back your first meal by 30 minutes every few days rather than jumping straight to noon. If you normally eat at 8am, start at 8:30. Then 9. Then 9:30. This gradual shift is much easier to sustain than cold turkey.

---

### SLIDE 6 — The muscle loss myth — why adequate protein is the answer

One of the most common concerns about intermittent fasting is muscle loss. People worry that fasting for extended periods means the body starts breaking down muscle for fuel.

The research is reassuring on this. Fasting periods of 16 to 24 hours do not cause significant muscle loss in people who are resistance training and eating adequate protein within their eating window. The body prioritises fat stores for fuel during fasting, not muscle tissue.

The key caveat is adequate protein. If you're fasting for 16 hours and then eating very little protein in your 8-hour window, you're not giving your body the raw materials it needs to maintain and build muscle. But that's a protein problem, not a fasting problem.

If you're hitting your protein target — 1.6 to 2 grams per kilogram per day — within your eating window, and you're resistance training, intermittent fasting will not cause muscle loss. You're fine.

---

### SLIDE 7 — How to decide if it's right for you

Here's a simple decision framework.

Try IF for four weeks if: you're not particularly hungry in the morning, you struggle with grazing and snacking throughout the day, and your training doesn't happen in the early morning hours.

Skip it or try it later if: you wake up genuinely hungry and skipping breakfast feels miserable, you train at high intensity first thing in the morning, you have a history of disordered eating, or you're managing a medical condition that requires regular food intake.

Remember: intermittent fasting is one tool. It's not better than other approaches — it's just different. Some people find it dramatically simplifies their relationship with food. Others find it makes them obsessive about mealtimes. Both responses are valid. If it doesn't work for you, that's data, not failure.

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 8 — Key takeaway

Intermittent fasting is a timing tool, not a magic system. It works by making it easier to eat fewer calories — nothing more. If it fits your life, use it. If it doesn't, you don't need it.
