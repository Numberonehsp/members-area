## ① HOOK

If you're new to training, or you've just ramped up the intensity after a break, there's a very specific moment you'll recognise.

It's the day after leg day. You go to sit down and your legs buckle. You try to walk down the stairs and seriously consider whether the ground floor is actually necessary. You feel like something has gone very wrong.

It hasn't. What you're experiencing is DOMS — and once you understand what it actually is, you'll never be frightened by it again.

### SLIDE 2 — What is DOMS?

DOMS stands for Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness. It's the muscle soreness and stiffness that typically appears 12 to 48 hours after exercise — not immediately during the session, but the next day or the day after.

The "delayed" part is important and often confuses people. You finish a training session feeling fine, go to bed, and wake up the next morning barely able to move. That's DOMS. The delay is what makes it feel like something went wrong after the fact.

What causes it? When you exercise — particularly when doing exercises that involve lowering weight or decelerating under load, known as eccentric movements — you create microscopic damage to the muscle fibres. Squats, deadlifts, lunges, bench press, and most resistance training movements all involve significant eccentric loading.

Your body responds to this damage with an inflammatory process — the same cellular machinery that responds to any tissue damage. Immune cells flood the area, inflammatory markers increase, and the repair process begins. It's this inflammation, and the associated fluid accumulation in the muscle tissue, that creates the sensation of soreness, stiffness, and the tenderness when you press on the muscle.

This is not injury. It's adaptation. The repair process builds the muscle back slightly stronger than before. DOMS is, in a very real sense, evidence that training is working.

### SLIDE 3 — Why beginners feel it more — and when it gets easier

If you're new to training, DOMS will be more intense than for experienced exercisers. This is normal and expected, and it gets meaningfully easier within a few weeks.

There are two reasons beginners feel DOMS more acutely.

First, your muscles aren't adapted to the mechanical stress of training yet. Experienced lifters have developed connective tissue, motor patterns, and cellular adaptations that reduce the microscopic damage from any given exercise. For a beginner, every new exercise is a novel stimulus, and the damage — and subsequent soreness — is correspondingly greater.

Second, you have no pain tolerance context yet. When a trained athlete has DOMS, they've experienced it hundreds of times. They know it passes, they know how bad it will get, they know they can train through most of it. When you experience it for the first time or second time, the uncertainty of "is this normal?" amplifies how unpleasant it feels.

The good news: the adaptation happens quickly. By your fourth to eighth week of consistent training, DOMS from routine sessions will be significantly reduced. Severe DOMS typically only returns when you introduce a genuinely new exercise pattern, significantly increase training volume, or return from a break of more than a week or two.

### SLIDE 4 — Nutrition for recovery: protein, anti-inflammatory foods, hydration

This is the module you're here for as part of the Nutrition pathway — because what you eat has a direct impact on how quickly and how well you recover.

**Protein** is the most important nutritional recovery factor. Your muscles are rebuilt from amino acids, which come from dietary protein. Without adequate protein — the 1.6 to 2 grams per kilogram target you know from Module 3 — the repair process is slowed. Getting protein right is the single most impactful nutritional decision you can make for recovery.

Timing matters modestly here. Having a protein-containing meal within two to three hours of your session helps supply amino acids when demand is highest. But as with the anabolic window myth covered in Module 5, total daily protein is the primary lever — don't stress over the exact timing.

**Anti-inflammatory foods** can support the recovery process. Oily fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) are rich in omega-3 fatty acids, which have well-documented anti-inflammatory effects. Berries, particularly tart cherry juice, have been studied specifically in the context of DOMS reduction with promising results. Leafy vegetables, olive oil, turmeric, and ginger all contain compounds with anti-inflammatory properties.

These aren't magic bullets — nothing nutritional will eliminate DOMS entirely — but a diet rich in these foods supports the recovery environment your body needs.

**Hydration** is underestimated for recovery. Muscle tissue is roughly 75 percent water. Dehydration impairs protein synthesis, reduces glycogen storage capacity, and increases the perceived intensity of DOMS. Aim for at least two to two and a half litres of water per day on training days, more if you sweat significantly or it's hot.

### SLIDE 5 — Active recovery vs passive rest — what actually speeds things up

When you're sore, the instinct is often to do nothing and wait it out. This is understandable but not optimal.

Research consistently shows that **active recovery** — light movement that promotes blood flow to sore muscles without adding significant new stress — reduces DOMS duration and perceived soreness compared to complete rest.

Active recovery options:
- A 20 to 30-minute low-intensity walk
- Light cycling at an easy pace
- Swimming at a comfortable pace
- Yoga or gentle stretching
- Foam rolling — there's moderate evidence for mild DOMS relief and improved range of motion, though it won't dramatically accelerate recovery

The mechanism is relatively simple: increased blood flow delivers more oxygen and nutrients to recovering muscle tissue and helps clear metabolic waste products. You're essentially speeding up the biological process that's already occurring.

**What doesn't meaningfully accelerate recovery from DOMS:**
- Ice baths have mixed evidence at best. Post-exercise cold immersion may actually blunt some of the adaptation signal, potentially slowing muscle growth over time if used routinely.
- Saunas — some evidence for general recovery but not specifically for DOMS reduction.
- Compression garments — modest evidence for reduced soreness perception, probably not worth buying specifically for DOMS.

The most accessible and evidence-backed recovery strategy: eat well, stay hydrated, get adequate sleep, and keep lightly moving.

### SLIDE 6 — Normal soreness vs warning signs (when to rest, when to push)

Most DOMS is safe to train through or around. But there are situations that warrant caution.

**Normal DOMS signs — safe to continue with modifications:**
- Soreness that is symmetrical (both legs, both arms)
- Stiffness that warms up and reduces after 10 to 15 minutes of movement
- Discomfort that peaks at 24 to 72 hours and then gradually reduces
- Soreness that doesn't worsen during light activity

**Signs to rest or seek advice:**
- Sharp or sudden pain during a movement — stop that exercise
- Joint pain rather than muscle soreness — joints should not be sore after training
- Soreness that is asymmetrical (one side significantly worse) and severe — could indicate a strain or injury
- Soreness that is getting worse after 72 hours rather than improving
- Swelling or significant bruising around a muscle or joint

**A rare but important note:** Extremely intense training by untrained individuals — particularly very long endurance events, military-style boot camps, or dramatically excessive volume — can cause a condition called rhabdomyolysis, where muscle breakdown releases proteins into the bloodstream that can damage the kidneys. Signs include very dark urine (brown or cola-coloured), severe swelling, and extreme weakness. This is rare but requires immediate medical attention.

For normal gym training at appropriate intensity levels, the risk of rhabdomyolysis is negligible. But it's worth knowing the warning signs.

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 7 — Key takeaway

DOMS is a sign that your body is adapting, not breaking. Support your recovery with adequate protein, hydration, anti-inflammatory foods, and light movement. Get better at distinguishing muscle soreness from joint pain, and train smart accordingly.
