# LESSON 1 — Carbs and fat myths
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Carbs make you fat. Fat makes you fat. So what exactly is making people fat?

The answer, of course, is eating more calories than you burn over time. Not a specific macronutrient. And yet the myths around carbs and fat are so entrenched that they continue to shape how millions of people eat — often in ways that make their diet worse, not better.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson we're dismantling the most persistent myths about carbohydrates and dietary fat — where they came from, why they're wrong, and what the evidence actually says.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Myth: Carbs make you fat

This is probably the most widespread nutrition myth of the last thirty years.

Here's why it feels true: when you reduce carbohydrates significantly, you lose weight quickly — often two to four kilograms in the first week. People see this and assume carbs were making them fat. But that weight loss is almost entirely water. Carbohydrates are stored as glycogen in your muscles and liver, and glycogen binds to water. Cut carbs, deplete glycogen, and you release that water. It's not fat loss — it's dehydration.

The research comparing low-carb and moderate-carb diets, when calories and protein are matched, consistently shows similar fat loss outcomes. The carbohydrate content of your diet is not the determining factor. Total calorie intake is.

Carbohydrates become a problem for body composition only when they come in highly processed, low-satiety forms that make it easy to eat large quantities without feeling full. A bag of crisps doesn't make you fat because it contains carbs — it makes you fat because it's easy to eat 600 calories of it without noticing. The issue is the calorie density and low satiety, not the macronutrient.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Myth: Fat makes you fat

This one has a more understandable origin. Fat contains 9 calories per gram — more than double protein or carbohydrate. So it seemed logical that eating fat would cause fat gain.

It doesn't. Like everything in nutrition, what matters is total calories. Dietary fat can contribute to fat gain if it pushes your total calorie intake above your TDEE — but so can protein, and so can carbohydrates. No macronutrient causes fat gain in isolation. Calorie surplus does.

The low-fat food movement of the 1980s and 90s, driven by this misunderstanding, produced decades of "healthy" low-fat products that were high in sugar and refined carbohydrates. Obesity rates rose throughout this period. Cutting fat and replacing it with sugar turned out not to be the solution.

Dietary fat is essential for hormones, vitamin absorption, cell membrane integrity, and brain function. Eating adequate fat from whole food sources is not just acceptable — it's necessary for health and performance.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Myth: Low-fat foods are healthy

Following directly from the last myth: low-fat labelling does not mean healthy.

When fat is removed from food, manufacturers typically add sugar, refined carbohydrates, thickeners, or artificial flavourings to compensate for the taste and texture that fat provides. Low-fat yoghurt, for example, is often comparable in calories to full-fat yoghurt but contains significantly more sugar and is less satiating — meaning you're likely to eat more overall.

The label "low-fat" is a marketing claim, not a health claim. The only meaningful question is: what is in this food, and how does it fit into my total daily intake?

When comparing two versions of the same product, look at total calories, protein, sugar, and ingredients — not just the fat content. Full-fat Greek yoghurt with a gram of sugar per 100g is almost always a better choice than low-fat yoghurt with 10 grams of sugar per 100g.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Neither carbohydrates nor fat make you fat in isolation. Eating more calories than you burn does. Low-fat labelling is marketing, not nutrition advice.

---

Your action step: next time you pick up a "low-fat" product, flip it over and compare it side by side with the full-fat version. Look at total calories and sugar content. You may be surprised what you find.

# LESSON 2 — Weight loss myths
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Weight loss is a multi-billion pound industry. And industries built on recurring customer revenue are not necessarily incentivised to give you information that actually solves your problem.

The result is a landscape full of myths, half-truths, and strategies that don't work — sold confidently to people who are genuinely trying to make progress.

Let's clear it up.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson we're debunking four of the most persistent weight loss myths: spot reduction, eating before bed, juice cleanses, and fasted cardio. By the end you'll know exactly which of these are worth your time.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Myth: Spot reduction — targeting fat in specific areas

This is the idea that you can lose fat from a specific part of your body by training that body part. Hundreds of sit-ups to lose belly fat. Inner thigh exercises to slim the thighs. Tricep kickbacks to lose arm fat.

Unfortunately, spot reduction does not work. It is not physiologically possible to tell your body where to mobilise fat from.

When you create a calorie deficit and your body needs to use stored fat for energy, it draws from fat stores throughout the body — not preferentially from the area you've been exercising. The pattern in which you lose fat is largely determined by genetics and hormones. Some people lose it first from their face and arms; others from their waist. You cannot directly control this.

What you can do is build muscle in a specific area while losing fat overall. This changes the shape and appearance of that area — it makes it more defined and muscular — but the fat loss happens across your whole body, not just there.

Conclusion: you cannot choose where you lose fat. You can choose where you build muscle. Focus on total body fat reduction through a calorie deficit, and let your genetics do the rest.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Myth: Eating before bed causes weight gain

You've probably heard this one: don't eat after 8pm. Eating at night goes straight to fat.

This is false. What causes fat gain is eating more calories than you burn over time — regardless of when those calories are consumed. Your body processes calories the same way at 9pm as it does at 9am. There is no metabolic switch that activates at night and starts storing everything as fat.

Where this myth originates from is a real observation. People who eat a lot late at night tend to gain more weight. But the cause isn't the timing — it's that late-night eating is often unplanned, calorie-dense snacking that pushes total daily intake well above maintenance. It's the extra calories, not the hour.

If your total calorie intake for the day is on target, having your last meal at 9pm instead of 7pm will not cause fat gain. Full stop.

That said, for some people, having a rule about not eating after a certain time is a useful tool to prevent evening snacking. If it helps you stay within your calorie target, use it. But understand it's a behavioural tool, not a biological necessity.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Myth: Juice cleanses detox your body and speed fat loss

Juice cleanses are one of the most profitable nutrition products in existence. They're also one of the least evidence-based.

The premise is that your body accumulates toxins from food, environment, and stress, and that consuming only fruit and vegetable juices for several days allows your body to "detox" and reset.

Your body does not accumulate toxins that require a juice cleanse to remove. You have a liver and kidneys whose entire job is to filter waste products from your blood and excrete them. They do this constantly, without any assistance from green juice. If they stopped doing this, you would be in a medical emergency within hours.

What juice cleanses actually do: they are very low calorie, so people lose weight rapidly — mostly water and glycogen, some fat. They feel different, sometimes better, because they've eliminated a lot of processed food, alcohol, and caffeine simultaneously. When they end, weight returns quickly as glycogen and water are restored.

There is no evidence that juice cleanses produce better fat loss outcomes than a standard calorie deficit, or that they provide any meaningful detoxification benefit beyond what your liver and kidneys do automatically.

If you want the benefits associated with cleanses — more vegetables, less processed food, less alcohol — eat more vegetables and less processed food. No £80 juice delivery required.

---

### SLIDE 5 — Myth: Fasted cardio burns more fat

This one has a kernel of truth — which is what makes it so persistent.

When you exercise in a fasted state, your body does preferentially burn fat for fuel because glycogen stores are lower. This is true. But — and this is the important part — this does not mean you lose more body fat over time.

Fat burning during exercise is not the same as total fat loss. What matters for fat loss is total calorie deficit over time, not the fuel source you use during any given workout.

When you exercise fasted, you may burn slightly more fat during that session. But you also tend to perform at a lower intensity (because glycogen is the primary fuel for high-intensity work), which means you may burn fewer total calories. And there's evidence that the body compensates for increased fat oxidation during fasted exercise by burning less fat later in the day.

Studies comparing fasted and fed cardio with matched calorie intake have found no meaningful difference in fat loss over several weeks.

The practical conclusion: do your cardio at the time of day when you perform best and enjoy it most. That's the session you'll do consistently, and consistency beats marginal effects every time.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 6 — Key takeaway

Spot reduction is a myth. Eating at night doesn't make you fat. Juice cleanses don't detox anything. Fasted cardio doesn't burn more fat overall. The fundamentals always win: calorie deficit, adequate protein, consistent training.

# LESSON 3 — Protein & supplement myths
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Protein has somehow attracted almost as many myths as it has benefits.

Despite being the most well-researched macronutrient for body composition, the myths around protein consumption are remarkably persistent — and some of them have genuinely stopped people from eating enough of it.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson we're tackling three of the biggest protein and supplement myths: that high protein damages your kidneys, that you must eat protein within 30 minutes of training, and that fat burners can do the heavy lifting for you.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Myth: High protein intake damages kidneys

This myth is probably the most medically harmful nutrition myth in common circulation, because it has caused perfectly healthy people to avoid adequate protein intake for fear of damaging their kidneys.

The claim originates from observations in patients with existing chronic kidney disease, where high protein intake can accelerate kidney deterioration. This is real and well-documented — for people who already have compromised kidney function.

In people with healthy kidneys, the research is clear: high protein intake does not cause kidney damage. Multiple studies have followed athletes and individuals eating two to three times the recommended daily allowance of protein over years and found no adverse kidney function outcomes in healthy individuals.

Your kidneys process protein metabolites efficiently as part of normal function. They are not overwhelmed by higher protein intake — they adapt and manage it. If you are healthy and your kidneys are functioning normally, eating 2 grams of protein per kilogram of body weight per day will not harm them.

If you have a history of kidney disease or are at risk, speak to your doctor before significantly increasing protein. For everyone else: this is not a reason to under-eat protein.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Myth: You must eat protein within 30 minutes of training

The "anabolic window" — the idea that you must consume protein within 30 minutes of training or you lose all the muscle-building benefit — was a dominant belief in gym culture for decades.

The science behind it is more nuanced than the myth suggests.

Muscle protein synthesis — the process of building new muscle tissue — is indeed elevated after resistance training and is supported by protein intake. But the window during which this elevation matters is much longer than 30 minutes. Research suggests that the relevant post-exercise period is several hours, not minutes.

More importantly, what matters most for muscle protein synthesis is your total daily protein intake. Hitting 1.6 to 2 grams per kilogram across the full day drives far better results than stressing over a 30-minute window.

A practical guideline: aim to have a protein-containing meal or snack within two to three hours of your session. That's a reasonable and research-backed goal. But if you train in the evening and eat dinner an hour later, you haven't missed a window and wasted your session. You're fine.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Myth: Fat burners work on their own

Fat burners are a category of supplement — typically capsules or powders — that claim to increase metabolism, accelerate fat loss, or suppress appetite. They are a multi-billion pound market globally.

The reality: the vast majority of fat burner supplements have little to no meaningful impact on fat loss when studied independently of diet and exercise.

Most fat burner formulas are centred on caffeine, which does modestly increase metabolic rate and can slightly improve training performance at relevant doses. That effect is real but small — and is available much more cheaply in a cup of coffee.

Other common ingredients — green tea extract, L-carnitine, capsaicin, synephrine — have some evidence of minor effects in isolation but those effects are typically negligible at realistic doses and do not translate to meaningful fat loss outcomes in practice.

Fat burners do not compensate for a poor diet. They cannot create a calorie deficit by themselves. And some contain stimulant combinations that can raise heart rate and blood pressure to concerning levels.

If you want to use caffeine before training for performance benefits, that's reasonable. But buying an expensive "fat burner" supplement expecting it to do the work your diet isn't doing is wasted money.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

High protein doesn't damage healthy kidneys. The post-workout window is hours, not minutes. Fat burners don't burn fat without a calorie deficit behind them. Total daily protein intake matters more than timing or supplements.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

Your action step: if you've been avoiding protein out of concern for your kidneys, or timing your meals frantically around workout windows, you can relax. Focus on hitting your total daily protein target. That's the lever that matters.

# LESSON 4 — Training myths
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Training myths are uniquely stubborn because they often contain a grain of truth — just enough to make them seem credible, and just distorted enough to cause real harm.

Some of them stop people from training effectively. Some of them cause unnecessary injury. And one of them — the last one we're covering — might be the most important piece of nutrition information in this entire module.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this final lesson we're dismantling four of the most persistent training myths: no pain no gain, women and bulking from lifting, sweating as a fat loss metric, and the idea that training can compensate for a bad diet.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Myth: No pain, no gain

This phrase has probably caused more training injuries than any other single idea in fitness.

There's a difference between discomfort and pain. Productive training involves discomfort — the burning sensation of a hard set, the breathlessness of an intense conditioning session, the muscular fatigue that builds over a workout. That discomfort is a signal that you're working hard enough to create an adaptation stimulus. It's normal and expected.

Sharp pain, joint pain, sudden pain, or pain that intensifies during or after an exercise is a different signal entirely. It's your body telling you that something is wrong. Training through that kind of pain risks turning a manageable issue into a serious injury.

The progress you make in training comes from progressive overload — gradually increasing the challenge to your body over time — combined with adequate recovery. Grinding through sessions in genuine pain does not accelerate this process. It typically sets it back by causing damage that prevents training.

Be honest about the difference. Chase discomfort. Respect pain.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Myth: Women will get bulky from lifting weights

This is possibly the most damaging myth in women's fitness, and it has kept a huge number of women away from resistance training — which is almost certainly the most beneficial form of exercise most people can do for body composition, bone density, metabolic health, and physical capability.

Here's the physiology. Building significant muscle mass — the kind associated with the word "bulky" — requires very high testosterone levels, a significant and sustained calorie surplus, years of consistent progressive resistance training, and in many cases, pharmacological assistance.

Women have roughly ten to thirty times less testosterone than men. Building muscle mass is significantly harder for women as a result. The women you see in fitness magazines or on social media who appear very muscular have almost always been training intensively for years, are in significant calorie surpluses, and in many cases are using performance-enhancing drugs.

What resistance training actually does for most women: it increases muscle definition, improves posture, increases metabolism (muscle tissue burns more calories at rest than fat tissue), strengthens bones and reduces osteoporosis risk, improves strength in everyday activities, and generally improves body composition — more muscle, less fat.

If you're a woman and you've been avoiding weights because of the bulking myth, this is your sign to start. You will not accidentally become bulky. It doesn't work that way.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Myth: Sweating means you're burning fat

This one is genuinely harmless as fitness myths go, but it's worth addressing because people make decisions based on it.

Sweating is your body's temperature regulation mechanism. You sweat to cool down. The amount you sweat depends on the intensity of your exercise, the ambient temperature, your fitness level, your genetics, and your hydration status.

Sweating a lot during a workout does not mean you burned more fat. It means you got hot.

The weight you lose immediately after a sweaty session is almost entirely water. Drink fluids and it comes back. It is not fat loss.

Fat loss happens over days and weeks through a sustained calorie deficit — not through sweat during a single session. If you're choosing exercise based on how much it makes you sweat, you're using the wrong metric. Choose exercise based on how much energy it requires, how sustainable it is, and how much you enjoy it.

---

### SLIDE 5 — Myth: You can out-train a bad diet

This is the most important myth in this lesson — and arguably in the whole module.

You cannot out-train a bad diet. Full stop.

Here's the arithmetic. A hard 45-minute weight training session burns roughly 250 to 350 calories for most people. A large fast food meal can contain 1,200 to 1,500 calories. Three protein bars eaten mindlessly throughout the day is another 600 calories. A large glass of wine is 200 calories. Two squares of chocolate becomes eight squares becomes 400 calories.

Exercise is exceptional for health, for body composition, for mental wellbeing, for longevity. It is not a calorie deficit machine. Even very high volumes of exercise cannot overcome consistently excessive calorie intake.

The people who make the best body composition progress are the ones who treat nutrition and training as equal partners — not the ones who train more to compensate for eating more.

This doesn't mean you need to be rigid or miserable about food. It means you need to build an honest picture of what you're eating and make genuine decisions about it — which is exactly what the first three modules of this pathway were designed to help you do.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 6 — Key takeaway

Discomfort is productive. Pain is a warning. Lifting won't make women bulky. Sweating isn't fat burning. And no amount of training will compensate for consistently eating too much. The kitchen is where most body composition results are made.
