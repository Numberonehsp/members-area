# LESSON 1 — What supplements actually are
**Quiz:** No

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Walk into any supplement shop and the walls are lined with products promising to accelerate fat loss, build muscle faster, boost your energy, sharpen your focus, enhance recovery, and optimise your hormones.

Most of them are unnecessary. Some of them are ineffective. A few of them are genuinely useful.

This module exists to tell you which is which — and to give you the framework to evaluate any new product you encounter, forever.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this first lesson, we're covering the foundational principles: what supplements actually are, why food comes first, and how the supplement industry is regulated. Once you understand these three things, marketing claims start to look very different.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — The food first principle

The single most important idea in this module: no supplement replaces food, and a poor diet cannot be supplemented into a good one.

Your body is designed to extract nutrients from whole food. Food provides nutrients in combinations, ratios, and forms that are absorbed and used effectively by your body. It also provides fibre, water, phytochemicals, and thousands of other compounds that researchers are still studying. A supplement provides an isolated compound — sometimes useful, but always inferior to getting that compound from real food.

Think of it this way: supplements can address a specific gap when food genuinely can't close it. If you're protein-deficient because your schedule makes hitting 160 grams of protein from whole food impractical, a protein shake is a sensible tool. If you're vegan and cannot get adequate B12 from diet alone, a B12 supplement is appropriate.

But supplements are not upgrades to good nutrition. They are patches for specific gaps. The foundation is always food.

A useful mental framework: before buying any supplement, ask yourself — "What is the gap in my diet or training that this is supposed to address?" If you can't articulate a specific, real gap, the supplement is probably unnecessary.

---

### SLIDE 3 — What "supplement" legally means and how the industry is regulated

Here's something most people don't know: in the UK, Europe, and the US, dietary supplements are not regulated in the same way as medicines.

Medicines must prove they are safe and effective before they can be sold. Supplements do not. They are treated more like food — the manufacturer is responsible for their safety, but independent pre-market testing is not required. A supplement can be sold making very general claims about supporting health without proving those claims in clinical trials.

What this means in practice:
- The label may not accurately reflect what's inside the product
- The amounts of each ingredient may differ from what's stated
- Products may be contaminated with other substances, including banned substances
- Marketing claims can be vague and non-verifiable

This doesn't mean all supplements are unsafe or fraudulent — most aren't. But it does mean you cannot assume a product is effective or clean simply because it's on sale.

For athletes in tested sports, this is particularly important. Contaminated supplements are one of the most common causes of inadvertent positive drug tests. If you compete in any tested sport, only use supplements that have been certified by a third-party testing organisation — in the UK, look for Informed Sport or NSF Certified for Sport certification.

For everyone else, buying from established, reputable brands reduces your risk significantly.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 4 — Key takeaway

Supplements are additions to a solid nutritional foundation — not replacements for one. They're not regulated like medicines. Before buying anything, identify the specific gap it's meant to address.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

Your action step: look at any supplements you currently use or are considering. For each one, write down the specific gap it's addressing. If you can't identify a clear gap, set it aside until after this module.

# LESSON 2 — Protein powder — do you need it?
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Protein powder is the gateway supplement. It's most people's first purchase, it dominates supplement shop shelf space, and it comes in more flavours and formulations than anyone could possibly need.

It's also one of the most straightforward supplements to evaluate. Let's cut through the noise.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you'll learn the difference between the main types of protein powder, when protein powder is genuinely useful and when it isn't, and how to choose a product without paying a premium for marketing.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Whey vs. casein vs. plant-based

The three main categories of protein powder reflect the protein source they're derived from.

**Whey protein** is derived from milk — specifically the liquid byproduct of cheese production. It's a complete protein, meaning it contains all nine essential amino acids your body can't produce itself. Whey is digested and absorbed quickly — typically within one to two hours — making it convenient post-workout. It has the highest leucine content of common protein powders, and leucine is the amino acid most directly associated with triggering muscle protein synthesis.

Whey comes in two main forms: **whey concentrate** (typically 70 to 80 percent protein by weight, contains some lactose and fat) and **whey isolate** (typically 90 percent or more protein, most lactose removed, lower fat). Isolate is better for those who are lactose intolerant. Concentrate is fine for most people and usually cheaper.

**Casein protein** is also derived from milk, but digested much more slowly — over four to eight hours. This makes it suitable before bed, where a slow release of amino acids through the night may support overnight muscle protein synthesis. The evidence for this benefit is modest but real.

**Plant-based proteins** — pea, rice, hemp, soy, and blends thereof — are suitable for vegans, vegetarians, or those with dairy intolerances. Soy is the only single-source plant protein that is complete on its own. Most plant protein powders blend sources (commonly pea and rice) to achieve a more complete amino acid profile. Plant proteins are generally slightly less bioavailable than whey, so the higher end of your protein target is worth aiming for.

---

### SLIDE 3 — When protein powder is useful — and when it isn't

Protein powder is useful in one specific situation: when you cannot practically meet your daily protein target from whole food sources alone.

Common scenarios where this applies:
- You train early in the morning and don't have time for a full protein-rich meal beforehand or immediately after
- Your work schedule makes eating enough protein at mealtimes consistently difficult
- You're plant-based and finding it challenging to reach 1.8 to 2g/kg from whole foods alone
- You travel frequently and whole food protein sources aren't reliably available

In these situations, protein powder is a practical, cost-effective solution. A shake with 25 to 35 grams of protein takes two minutes and significantly helps close the gap.

Protein powder is not useful in the following situations:
- You're already hitting your protein target from food and just think more protein is better (it isn't, beyond your daily target)
- You're buying it because it's marketed as a workout product (protein powder is just protein — there's no training magic in it)
- You're using it as a meal replacement in ways that are reducing your overall food quality and satisfaction

The simplest test: track your protein for one week without using any supplements. If you consistently hit 1.6 to 2g/kg from food, you don't need protein powder. If you regularly fall 30 or more grams short, a daily shake is a sensible addition.

---

### SLIDE 4 — How to choose a protein powder

The supplement industry charges a significant premium for flavour, branding, and marketing. Here's how to cut through that.

Look at the label. The most important number is protein per serving. You want at least 20 to 25 grams of protein per serving, ideally more.

Check the ingredients list. The protein source should be the first ingredient — whey concentrate, whey isolate, pea protein, etc. Be cautious of products where the protein source is buried behind a long list of other ingredients, or where the label lists a "protein blend" with impressive-sounding names that may not have strong evidence.

Look for third-party testing if you're in any tested sport. Informed Sport or NSF certification on the label is the minimum standard.

Ignore the marketing claims. "Advanced formula," "rapid absorption matrix," "anabolic catalyst technology" — none of these terms mean anything specific or regulated. Focus on the numbers.

Price comparison tip: divide the total cost by the number of servings to get cost per serving. A bigger tub is almost always better value than a smaller one from the same brand. And reputable own-brand options from established nutrition companies are frequently identical in quality to premium branded versions at a fraction of the price.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Protein powder is a convenient way to close a protein gap when food can't do it. Whey is the best-studied option for most people. If you're hitting your protein target from food, you don't need it.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

Your action step: track your protein for three days without using any supplements. Calculate the daily average and compare it to your 1.8g/kg target. That gap — if there is one — tells you whether protein powder would actually help.

# LESSON 3 — Creatine — the most researched supplement
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

If there is one supplement that the sports science community broadly agrees is safe and effective, it's creatine.

It's also one of the most misunderstood supplements in common use. People associate it with bulk, with kidney damage, with water retention, with things that don't entirely reflect how it works. Let's fix that.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you'll understand what creatine actually is and how it works in the body, how much to take and how, and what the water retention effect actually means and whether it's something to worry about.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — ATP production and what creatine does

To understand creatine, you need to briefly understand how muscles produce energy.

Your muscles use a molecule called ATP — adenosine triphosphate — as their immediate energy currency. When a muscle contracts, ATP is broken down to release energy. The problem is that your muscles can only store a very small amount of ATP at any one time — enough for roughly two to three seconds of maximal effort.

For short, explosive efforts — a heavy squat, a sprint, a jump — your body regenerates ATP using a stored compound called phosphocreatine. This system is fast but rapidly depleted. After about eight to twelve seconds of maximal effort, phosphocreatine stores are largely exhausted and your body switches to slower energy systems.

Here's where supplemental creatine comes in. When you take creatine monohydrate consistently, you increase the phosphocreatine stores in your muscles. More stored phosphocreatine means your muscles can regenerate ATP faster, sustain maximal or near-maximal effort slightly longer, and recover between sets slightly more quickly.

In practice this translates to: an extra rep or two on your hard sets, slightly better recovery between sets, marginally improved sprint and high-intensity performance. Over weeks and months of training, those marginal improvements accumulate into meaningfully greater training volume and adaptation — more muscle, more strength, better performance.

Creatine doesn't directly build muscle. It improves the quality of your training sessions, which builds muscle over time. That distinction matters.

---

### SLIDE 3 — 3–5g/day, no loading required

The standard recommendation for creatine supplementation is 3 to 5 grams of creatine monohydrate per day, taken consistently.

You may have heard about a loading phase — taking 20 grams per day for the first five to seven days to saturate your muscles quickly. Loading works and will get you to saturation faster, but it's not necessary. Taking 3 to 5 grams per day will bring your muscle creatine stores to the same saturated level in three to four weeks. The only advantage of loading is getting there faster. The long-term outcome is identical.

For most people, especially beginners, there is no reason to load. Just take 3 to 5 grams daily, consistently, and you'll be fully saturated within a month.

Timing is largely irrelevant. Taking creatine post-workout has a modest advantage in some studies but the effect is small. Taking it consistently is far more important than taking it at a specific time. Many people simply add it to their morning drink or protein shake.

Creatine monohydrate — the plain, unbranded form — is the most studied and is the standard against which all other creatine formulations are compared. Other forms (creatine HCl, buffered creatine, kre-alkalyn) are typically more expensive and have no proven advantage over plain monohydrate. Buy the cheapest creatine monohydrate from a reputable brand.

---

### SLIDE 4 — The kidney myth and water retention explained

Two concerns come up most often with creatine, and both deserve honest answers.

**The kidney myth.** Like the high-protein myth covered in Module 5, the idea that creatine damages kidneys originated from observations in people with existing kidney problems. In people with healthy kidneys, decades of research — including long-term studies — have found no evidence of kidney damage from creatine supplementation at normal doses.

Creatine metabolism produces creatinine, a waste product that is filtered by the kidneys. People taking creatine will have higher creatinine levels on a blood test, which can look alarming if a doctor isn't aware you're supplementing. It's worth mentioning to any healthcare provider you see. But elevated creatinine from creatine supplementation is not the same as kidney damage — it simply reflects higher creatine turnover.

If you have existing kidney disease or reduced kidney function, speak to your doctor before starting creatine.

**Water retention.** This one is real, and it's worth understanding rather than being concerned about.

Creatine is stored in muscle tissue with water. When your muscle creatine stores increase, they draw water into the muscle cells. This typically results in a weight gain of one to two kilograms in the first week or two of supplementation. This is intramuscular water — it's inside the muscle cells, not under the skin. It doesn't make you look puffy or soft. It is simply your muscles holding more water because they're more energy-rich.

Over time this intramuscular water is associated with slightly fuller-looking muscles. Most people consider this a positive effect.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Creatine monohydrate is safe, effective, and one of the most studied supplements in existence. Take 3 to 5 grams daily, consistently. Expect one to two kilograms of water weight initially. It won't damage your kidneys.

---

## ⑤ ACTION STEP + NEXT

Your action step: if you do resistance training and want a meaningful, evidence-backed supplement, creatine monohydrate is the recommendation. Buy the cheapest plain monohydrate from a reputable brand — 500 grams will last several months.

# LESSON 4 — Pre-workout & fat burners
**Quiz:** Yes

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide
*(No script — title card only.)*

---

## ① HOOK

Pre-workout supplements are one of the highest-growth categories in the supplement industry. Fat burners are one of the highest-margin.

Both are heavily marketed. Both are frequently misrepresented. And both deserve a clear-eyed look at what they actually do — and what they don't.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

In this lesson you'll understand what's actually in most pre-workout products, why caffeine dependency is a real consideration, what fat burners can and can't do, and who should avoid stimulant-based supplements entirely.

---

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — What pre-workout contains

Pre-workout supplements are designed to enhance training performance — more energy, better focus, improved endurance, stronger muscle pumps.

The most important ingredient in almost every pre-workout formula is **caffeine**. Typically 150 to 300 milligrams per serving, sometimes more. Caffeine is a well-studied performance enhancer. At doses of roughly 3 to 6mg per kilogram of body weight, it has clear evidence for improving endurance performance, reducing perceived exertion, increasing pain tolerance during training, and modestly improving strength and power output. The caffeine in pre-workout is doing most of the work.

Other common ingredients include:

**Beta-alanine** — an amino acid that increases muscle carnosine levels, which helps buffer acid during high-rep or endurance work. The evidence is moderate and specific to activities over 60 seconds. Beta-alanine causes a harmless tingling sensation in the skin (paresthesia) — this is the "tingly" feeling often attributed to "feeling the pre-workout working." It's just beta-alanine, not a sign of effectiveness.

**Citrulline malate** — an amino acid that supports nitric oxide production and blood flow. There is reasonable evidence for modest endurance and pump benefits at doses of 6 to 8 grams. Many pre-workouts underdose this significantly.

**Creatine** — some pre-workouts include creatine. This is fine, but you need to be taking a consistent daily dose (3 to 5g) for it to work — a pre-workout creatine dose is not meaningful on its own.

The reality is that most people could replicate the benefits of a pre-workout with a cup of strong coffee, 10 grams of citrulline malate, and a gram of beta-alanine — all cheaper than a branded pre-workout formula.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Caffeine dependency and the tolerance problem

Here's the issue with using pre-workout products daily or even several times a week: caffeine tolerance builds quickly.

Your body adapts to regular caffeine exposure by downregulating the adenosine receptors that caffeine blocks. Within two to three weeks of daily use at the same dose, the perceived energy and focus boost significantly diminishes. People respond by taking more, or choosing stronger products. Eventually, the pre-workout isn't giving you extra energy — it's just preventing you from feeling the withdrawal symptoms of not having it.

At this point, you're not performing better with pre-workout. You're performing the same without it.

Practical strategies to avoid this:
- Save pre-workout for sessions where you genuinely need it — heavy lifting days, early morning sessions, important training sessions
- Take regular breaks — one to two weeks off caffeine every two to three months helps restore sensitivity
- Or simply use coffee as your primary caffeine source, which naturally limits your intake to two to three cups rather than unlimited supplement servings

If you're currently drinking coffee throughout the day and then taking a 300mg caffeine pre-workout before training, you're consuming potentially 500 to 600mg of caffeine daily. That's approaching the range where sleep quality, anxiety, and cardiovascular effects become real considerations.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Fat burner myths and who should avoid stimulants

We covered fat burners briefly in Module 5's myths lesson, but it's worth being more specific here.

The core claim of fat burner supplements is that they increase your metabolic rate and/or fat oxidation to a degree that produces meaningful fat loss beyond what a calorie deficit alone would produce. The evidence does not support this claim for any commercially available fat burner at normal doses.

Caffeine-containing fat burners do mildly increase metabolic rate — by perhaps 50 to 100 calories per day at typical doses. This is real but small. It's also temporary — the effect diminishes as tolerance builds.

Beyond caffeine, common fat burner ingredients like green tea extract, L-carnitine, synephrine, and capsaicin have modest evidence of minor effects that do not translate to meaningful fat loss in practice. None of them create a fat loss effect that approaches the impact of a modest dietary change.

More importantly, some fat burner products contain stimulant combinations — synephrine, yohimbine, or proprietary blends — that can significantly elevate heart rate and blood pressure. For most healthy adults, this is an unpleasant side effect. For people with cardiovascular conditions, hypertension, anxiety disorders, or those sensitive to stimulants, these products carry genuine health risks.

**Who should avoid stimulant-based supplements entirely:**
- Anyone with a heart condition or arrhythmia
- Anyone with high blood pressure
- Anyone with an anxiety disorder or panic attacks
- Pregnant or breastfeeding women
- Anyone under 18
- Anyone sensitive to caffeine effects

The bottom line on fat burners: save your money. A calorie deficit does more than any fat burner, without the side effects and expense.

---

## ④ KEY TAKEAWAY

### SLIDE 5 — Key takeaway

Pre-workout works primarily through caffeine. Use it selectively to avoid tolerance build-up. Fat burners don't burn meaningful amounts of fat. Stimulant-based supplements carry real risks for sensitive individuals and people with cardiovascular or anxiety conditions.

# LESSON 5 — Reading labels & spotting marketing nonsense
**Quiz:** No

---

## SLIDE 1 — Title slide

## ① HOOK

By this point you know the evidence behind the major supplement categories. But the supplement industry moves fast — new products, new ingredients, new claims appear constantly.

In this final lesson I'm going to give you a framework for evaluating any supplement you encounter — now and in the future — so you can make an informed decision without doing hours of research every time.

---

## ② SIGNPOST

You're going to learn what proprietary blends actually are and why they should raise a flag, what third-party certification means and why it matters, and the red flag phrases that signal marketing over science.

## ③ CONTENT

---

### SLIDE 2 — Proprietary blends — what they are and why they matter

A proprietary blend is a group of ingredients listed on a supplement label as a single combined amount — rather than each ingredient having its own stated dose.

For example, a label might say "Performance Blend: 3,000mg" and then list caffeine, beta-alanine, citrulline, taurine, and several other ingredients — but not tell you how much of each is in there.

This matters because effective doses of each ingredient vary enormously. Citrulline malate requires 6 to 8 grams to have a meaningful effect. Beta-alanine requires 2 to 5 grams. Caffeine requires 150mg or more. If a blend totals 3,000mg and contains ten ingredients, it's mathematically impossible for most of them to be at an effective dose.

Manufacturers use proprietary blends for two reasons. First, to protect their formulas from being directly copied by competitors. Second — and more commonly — to include very small amounts of expensive or well-known ingredients for label marketing purposes without needing to actually use an effective dose.

When you see a proprietary blend, you cannot evaluate whether the product contains enough of any ingredient to actually work. That should factor into your purchase decision. Prefer products that list each ingredient with its individual dose.

---

### SLIDE 3 — Banned substance testing — what certifications to look for

If you compete in any organised sport with drug testing — whether that's powerlifting, athletics, martial arts, cycling, or any professional sport — supplement contamination is a real and serious risk.

Supplement manufacturing facilities are not always dedicated facilities. Cross-contamination from other products can introduce banned substances into a product that has no intention of containing them. Athletes have received bans and had careers damaged by contaminated supplements.

The two main third-party certification programmes to look for:

**Informed Sport** (UK-based, widely accepted internationally) — supplements carrying this logo are batch-tested for substances banned under the World Anti-Doping Agency (WADA) list. Every batch, not just initial testing.

**NSF Certified for Sport** (US-based, widely accepted in professional sport) — similar rigorous batch testing programme.

Both certifications significantly reduce but do not eliminate contamination risk. They are the minimum standard for any athlete in a tested sport.

If you don't compete in tested sport, these certifications are still a useful indicator of quality and accurate labelling — but they're not as critical a requirement.

---

### SLIDE 4 — Red flag phrases and ingredients to watch for

Here's a quick reference for phrases and patterns that should prompt more scrutiny before buying.

**Red flag phrases:**
- "Clinically proven" — real clinical evidence is cited with specific studies. If there's no reference and no specific claim, it's marketing.
- "Breakthrough formula" / "revolutionary technology" — these are advertising words with no regulatory meaning.
- "As used by professional athletes" — endorsements don't validate efficacy.
- "Detoxifying" / "cleansing" — if applied to anything other than the liver and kidneys doing their normal job, this has no scientific basis.
- "Boosts metabolism naturally" — this phrase appears on products whose metabolic effect ranges from nil to negligible.
- "Thermogenic" — a real phenomenon (the thermic effect of food), but the word is used so loosely in marketing that it conveys almost nothing specific.

**Ingredients that warrant additional scrutiny at high doses or in certain populations:**
- Yohimbine — stimulant with anxiety and cardiovascular effects; common in fat burners
- DMAA / DMHA — stimulants that have been banned or restricted in multiple countries; found in some pre-workouts. Avoid.
- Synephrine (bitter orange extract) — stimulant often marketed as a safer alternative to ephedrine; cardiovascular effects at high doses

**Useful signals of a trustworthy product:**
- Full ingredient disclosure with individual doses
- Third-party testing certification (Informed Sport, NSF)
- Established company with verifiable history
- Simple formulation — effectiveness doesn't require a thirty-ingredient label
- No extravagant claims on the packaging
